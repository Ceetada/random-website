Why I’m starting this blog

by Your Name

For years I’ve had the same thought every few weeks: I should write that down somewhere. Not for anyone in particular — just somewhere that isn’t a note-taking app I’ll abandon by spring. This is that somewhere.

I want it to be small on purpose. No metrics I check, no schedule I resent, no growth strategy. A post goes up when I have something worth keeping, and not a moment before.

What this is

Mostly short essays and notes — the kind of thing I’d tell a friend over coffee and then wish I’d remembered later. Some of it will be about work, most of it won’t.

Write the post you’d want to re-read in a year, not the one that does well this week.

That’s the whole plan. If you’re reading this, thanks for stopping by — there’s more below whenever you want it.

On keeping a paper notebook

by Your Name

My phone can capture a thought in two seconds, sync it to every device I own, and remind me about it next Tuesday. My notebook can do none of that. I keep reaching for the notebook.

Part of it is friction, in a good way. Writing by hand is slow enough that I only bother with the thoughts that survive the trip from my head to the page. The bad ideas quietly don’t make it.

The margins are the point

A page has room to be messy — an arrow here, a box around the thing that matters, a question mark I’ll answer later. Apps make that hard. Paper just shrugs and lets me.

I’m not precious about it. If you love your app, keep your app. But if you’ve never given a cheap notebook a real week, it might surprise you.

Everyday June 24, 2026

The best cup of coffee I made this year

by Your Name

It wasn’t a special bean. It wasn’t new gear. It was a Tuesday morning with nowhere to be for twenty minutes, which turns out to be the rarest ingredient in my kitchen.

I ground the beans I already had. I paid attention to the water this time — off the boil, poured slow. I watched it bloom instead of scrolling. That was the whole trick.

The gear was never the bottleneck. My attention was.

I think about that more than I’d like to admit. Half the things I want to do “better” don’t need better tools. They need me to slow down enough to do them properly once.
